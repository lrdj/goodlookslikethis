All the talks were recorded and will be [posted in the archive](https://www.nexerdigital.com/camp-digital/archive/) soon. It's worth taking a look even before this year's events show up as there are some really brilliant earlier talks there (Lou Downe on [why services fail](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=quchZlTM2Cs) back in 2024 is a favourite).

I didn't bring a laptop and didn't take notes so thought I'd mention some moments or ideas that really stood out for me. 

Here are three things I felt — ideas I heard...

## Rachel Coldicutt on Hope

- Reality has been distorted and problems hidden by technology giants operating at a scale that makes accountability almost impossible. The damage is real but it is also frequently invisible — until it isn't.
- Many of the loudest voices in tech — those doing the most complaining about how broken everything is — are white, male, and privileged. The irony of that posture tends to go unexamined.
- Hope is not naïve and it is not passive. Rachel's point was that hope is actually contagious — it only takes concerted action by a relatively small number of people for it to start spreading to those around them. You don't need a majority. You need a committed, visible few. This is a genuine cause for optimism:-)

## Himal Mandalia on the intersection of commitment and burnout

- When capable people step in to fix broken systems, they inadvertently teach those systems that the problem is being handled. The incentive to actually fix the underlying issue evaporates. The people absorb the dysfunction so the organisation doesn't have to.
- This means the negative impact on the humans doing the fixing quietly compounds. The system is fine. The people are not.
- The key concept Himal introduced — and one that landed hard given my current context — is taking responsibility without authority. The people who care most about the work are precisely those most likely to step in and carry a burden that isn't formally theirs to carry. They do it because they care, not because anyone asked them to. Over time, that gap between responsibility and authority becomes a significant and underacknowledged cause of burnout. It's worth sitting with that for a while.

## Tessa Quinn on doing the right thing against formidable odds

Tessa told us the story of Scotland's "Super Sponsor" scheme — the intention of which was to allow Ukrainians fleeing the war to apply for a visa naming the Scottish Government as their sponsor, meaning they didn't need a pre-arranged host. A small but significant design decision that simplified the process and meaningfully improved safeguarding at a moment of genuine crisis.

It's an extraordinary story. Worth [reading more on scotgov](https://www.gov.scot/publications/scotlands-support-displaced-people-ukraine/) for the full picture. Several things from Tessa's talk really hit home — to the point where I found myself unexpectedly emotional at one point (long story; involves Covid and Nicola Sturgeon, which some of you will understand).

Three things stayed with me:

**Emotional safety under pressure.** The team was operating in genuinely traumatic conditions — the subject matter was war, displacement, families separated. Tessa talked about how deliberately cultivating psychological safety within the team wasn't a nice-to-have, it was operationally essential. People needed to be able to say when something wasn't working, raise doubts, flag distress — and know they'd be heard. Without that, the pace they were working at would have broken people much faster.

**Radical adaptability.** This was not a programme with a comfortable runway. It was weeks, sometimes days. The team used whatever tools, platforms, and people were to hand — patching things together in ways that would make a procurement officer weep, but that actually worked. The "Warm Scots Welcome" app, used to match displaced people to hosts and housing across Scotland, came out of that environment. It's a vivid example of what's possible when you stop waiting for the perfect conditions and start with what you've got.

**Leadership as moral clarity.** This wasn't just a job. It was the visible, practical expression of Nicola Sturgeon's very public position on what it means to be Scottish — the "what would Scotland do?" question answered in real time. That framing, coming from the top with genuine conviction, had a remarkable effect on the team. It made the work almost self-motivating. People weren't just executing a programme; they were manifesting a value. Senior leaders being seen to personally mean it — not just say it — changes what teams believe is possible.

---

Three very different talks, but they shared a thread. Rachel reminded us that hope requires action, not just sentiment. Himal showed us where commitment without structural support leads. And Tessa demonstrated what it looks like when a team has the emotional foundation, the adaptability, and the moral clarity to do something genuinely remarkable under impossible conditions. 

Good day out in Manchester.
