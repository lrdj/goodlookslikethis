All the talks were recorded and will be [posted in the archive](https://www.nexerdigital.com/camp-digital/archive/) soon. It's worth taking a look even before this year's events show up as there are some really brilliant earlier talks there (Lou Downe on [why services fail](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=quchZlTM2Cs) back in 2024 is a favourite).

I didn't bring a laptop and didn't take notes so thought I'd mention some moments or ideas that really stood out for me. Here are three things I felt - ideas I heard...

## Rachel Coldecutt on Hope:

- Reality has been distorted and problems hidden by uncountable technology giants 
- Many 'technologists' are white, male, privileged and yet are complaining that everything is terrible
- Hope is contagious. It only takes concerted action by a relatively small number for hope to start infecting those around them. This is a cause for optimism:-)

## Himal Mandalia on the intersection of commitment and burnout

- People step in to fix things so systems learn that there is a problem
- This means the negative impact on teams of humans continues
- Key concept (relevant to my current work) the idea of taking responsibility without authority. Leaders and stronger practitioners are often those that care more about the work so will often step in to carry the burden. Responsibility without authority is major cause of burnout.

## Tessa Quinn on doing the right thing against formidable odds

Tessa told us the story of Scotland's "Super Sponsor" scheme the intension of which was to allow Ukrainians to apply for a visa naming the Scottish Government as their sponsor — meaning they didn't need a pre-arranged host, simplifying the process and improving safeguarding.

It's an amazing story that tells of a team coming together and inventing technology and processes out of thin air with only days and weeks to act. Worth [reading the full story](https://www.gov.scot/publications/ukraine-resettlement-stakeholder-reference-group-minutes-june-2023/) on scotgov.

Several parts of Tessa's talk really hit home. To the point where I was triggered and started blubbering at one point (long story, involves Covid and Nicola Sturgeon).


On arrival, people were met at "Welcome Hubs", triaged, offered care and support, and placed in temporary accommodation until longer-term housing could be arranged. A matching service sought to place people with Scottish households who had undergone property and disclosure checks. gov.scot
The digital layer Tessa owned included the "Warm Scots Welcome" app — used by local authorities and a Scottish Government surge team to match people in temporary accommodation to hosts or suitable housing across Scotland. Scottish Parliament
Tessa's role was formally "Design, Digital and Data Service Owner (Ukraine)" at the Scottish Government — so she was across the full data/digital stack, not just one layer. gov.scot

The scale and pressure

The Ukraine resettlement team at the Scottish Government won the excellence in delivery award at the civil service awards — so it was recognised internally as exceptional delivery under pressure. Scottish Parliament
Ukranian resettlement — Tessa's Ukrainian resettlement talk clearly had some solid practical principles frged like hard-won lessons from delivery under genuine pressure, not theoretical design principles. The Ukrainian resettlement context would have given these real weight.

Get the data right

- Stabilise data before building on it — classic crisis delivery trap, you reach for the build before the data model is solid
- Data security paramount but easily overlooked — especially under pressure when speed feels more urgent
- Data needed for operations and decision-making — two different audiences, often underserved by the same dataset
- Help the business/customer own its data — interesting framing for a government service, pushes accountability outward

Expect change

- Tight scope then iterate — sensible, but hard to hold politically when stakeholders want the full vision delivered yesterday
- Design for change, build in flexibility — resonates strongly with One Login's challenge of serving wildly different relying parties
- "If you're not sure it's right, make sure it's not wrong" — that's a keeper. Cautious progress over confident mistakes
- Map your service at high level to show dependencies — dependency mapping as a communication tool as much as a design one
- "Perfect is the enemy of the good" — attributed to her Dad, which is a nice touch
